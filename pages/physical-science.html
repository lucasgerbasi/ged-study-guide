Physical Science

Physical Science Study Guide

I. Matter and Its Properties

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. Understanding the properties and behaviors of matter is essential for Physical Science.

1. States of Matter

  • Solid: Has a fixed shape and volume. Molecules are tightly packed and vibrate.
  • Liquid: Has a fixed volume but no fixed shape. Molecules are close but can move past each other.
  • Gas: Has neither fixed volume nor shape. Molecules are far apart and move freely.
  • Plasma: An ionized gas, found in stars and lightning.

2. Physical vs. Chemical Properties

  • Physical Properties: Can be observed without changing the substance (e.g., color, density, melting point, boiling point).
  • Chemical Properties: Describes a substance's ability to undergo a chemical change (e.g., flammability, reactivity with water).

3. Changes in Matter

  • Physical Changes: Changes in appearance, but not composition (e.g., melting, freezing).
  • Chemical Changes: Result in the formation of a new substance (e.g., rusting, burning).

II. Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table

Atoms are the fundamental building blocks of matter, composed of protons, neutrons, and electrons.

1. Atomic Structure

  • Protons: Positive charge, located in the nucleus.
  • Neutrons: Neutral charge, located in the nucleus.
  • Electrons: Negative charge, orbit around the nucleus in electron shells.

2. The Periodic Table

  • Elements are arranged by increasing atomic number.
  • Periods: Horizontal rows indicate the number of electron shells.
  • Groups/Families: Vertical columns indicate elements with similar chemical properties.
  • Metals: Conduct heat and electricity, are malleable and ductile.
  • Nonmetals: Poor conductors, brittle.
  • Metalloids: Have properties of both metals and nonmetals.

3. Isotopes

Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons. Example: Carbon-12 and Carbon-14.

III. Chemical Bonding and Reactions

Chemical bonding and reactions are crucial for understanding how substances combine and react.

1. Types of Chemical Bonds

  • Ionic Bonds: Formed when electrons are transferred between atoms, resulting in charged ions (e.g., sodium chloride, NaCl).
  • Covalent Bonds: Formed when electrons are shared between atoms (e.g., water, H₂O).
  • Metallic Bonds: In metals, electrons are shared in a "sea" of electrons, allowing metals to conduct electricity and heat.

2. Balancing Chemical Equations

Law of Conservation of Mass: Matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.

3. Types of Chemical Reactions

  • Synthesis: Two or more substances combine to form a more complex product (e.g., A + B → AB).
  • Decomposition: A compound breaks down into simpler substances (e.g., AB → A + B).
  • Single Replacement: One element replaces another in a compound (e.g., AB + C → AC + B).
  • Double Replacement: Two compounds exchange ions (e.g., AB + CD → AD + CB).
  • Combustion: A substance reacts with oxygen, often producing heat and light (e.g., burning of fuel).

IV. Motion and Forces (Physics)

Physics explains how objects move and interact with forces.

1. Newton's Laws of Motion

  • First Law (Inertia): An object will remain at rest or in motion unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Second Law (F = ma): Force equals mass times acceleration.
  • Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

2. Types of Forces

  • Gravitational Force: The attraction between masses (e.g., Earth's pull on objects).
  • Friction: Resistance that opposes motion.
  • Tension: The force transmitted through a rope or string.
  • Normal Force: The support force exerted by a surface, perpendicular to the object.

3. Work and Energy

  • Work: The transfer of energy when a force moves an object (W = F × d).
  • Kinetic Energy: Energy of motion (KE = 1/2 mv²).
  • Potential Energy: Energy stored in an object due to its position (PE = mgh).
  • Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

4. Momentum

Momentum: The product of an object's mass and velocity (p = mv). Conservation of Momentum: In a closed system, total momentum before and after an interaction remains the same.

V. Heat and Temperature

Heat is the energy transferred between substances at different temperatures.

1. Heat Transfer Methods

  • Conduction: Heat transfer through direct contact (e.g., a metal spoon in hot water).
  • Convection: Heat transfer through the movement of fluids (e.g., boiling water).
  • Radiation: Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves (e.g., heat from the Sun).

2. Temperature

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles. Measured in Celsius (°C), Fahrenheit (°F), or Kelvin (K).

3. Thermodynamics

  • First Law: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred.
  • Second Law: Heat flows from hot to cold objects.
  • Third Law: As temperature approaches absolute zero, the entropy of a system approaches a minimum.

VI. Waves and Sound (Physics)

Waves are disturbances that transfer energy through a medium (solid, liquid, or gas).

1. Properties of Waves

  • Wavelength: The distance between two consecutive points on a wave (e.g., crest to crest).
  • Frequency: The number of waves that pass a point per second (measured in Hertz, Hz).
  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement from the rest position; determines the wave’s energy.
  • Speed: The rate at which a wave travels (v = f × λ).

2. Sound Waves

Sound waves are longitudinal waves that require a medium to travel through (solid, liquid, or gas). Pitch: The perceived frequency of sound. Volume: The perceived amplitude of sound.

VII. Electricity and Magnetism (Physics)

Electricity and magnetism are interrelated, forming the basis of electromagnetism.

1. Electricity

  • Electric Current: The flow of electric charge (measured in amperes, A).
  • Voltage: The potential difference that drives the current (measured in volts, V).
  • Resistance: The opposition to current flow (measured in ohms, Ω).
  • Ohm’s Law: V = IR, where V is voltage, I is current, and R is resistance.

2. Circuits

  • Series Circuit: Components connected in a single path, where current flows through each component.
  • Parallel Circuit: Components connected in multiple paths, allowing current to flow through more than one path.

3. Magnetism

  • Magnetic Fields: Regions around magnets where magnetic forces can be detected.
  • Electromagnetic Induction: The process where a changing magnetic field can induce an electric current in a conductor.
