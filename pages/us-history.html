U.S. History

1. Colonial History and Early American Settlements

The Thirteen Colonies

  • The English Colonies: Jamestown (1607), Plymouth Colony (1620), and others.
  • Economic Foundations: Agricultural economy, Triangular Trade, and Slavery.

2. The American Revolution (1775-1783)

Causes of the Revolution

  • Taxation Without Representation, Intolerable Acts, and Enlightenment Ideas.

Major Events

  • The Battles of Lexington and Concord (1775), Declaration of Independence (1776), Battle of Saratoga (1777), and Siege of Yorktown (1781).

The Treaty of Paris (1783)

  • Officially ended the war, recognizing U.S. independence and granting territorial gains.

3. The Constitution and Bill of Rights

  • Articles of Confederation and the drafting of the U.S. Constitution (1787).
  • Key Principles: Separation of Powers, Checks and Balances, and Federalism.
  • The Bill of Rights (1791) secured individual freedoms such as free speech, and protection from unlawful searches.

4. Expansion and Manifest Destiny

  • The Louisiana Purchase (1803), Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804-1806), and the idea of Manifest Destiny.
  • The Mexican-American War (1846-1848), resulting in U.S. territorial gains in the Southwest.

5. Civil War and Reconstruction (1861-1877)

  • Causes of the Civil War: Slavery, States' Rights, and Election of Abraham Lincoln.
  • Major Events: Battle of Gettysburg (1863), Emancipation Proclamation (1863), and Appomattox Court House (1865).
  • Reconstruction: 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments, aimed at rebuilding the South and integrating formerly enslaved people.

6. The Industrial Revolution and Gilded Age

  • The rise of factories, mass production, and figures like Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, and J.P. Morgan.
  • The growth of cities, labor movements, and increased immigration during the late 1800s.
  • The Gilded Age: A period of economic growth, but also political corruption and poor working conditions.

7. The Progressive Era and Early 20th Century

  • Progressivism, with reforms such as women's suffrage (19th Amendment), labor rights, and the establishment of federal agencies.
  • World War I (1914-1918): U.S. entry due to German actions, and the establishment of the League of Nations.

8. The Great Depression and New Deal

  • The Stock Market Crash (1929) and the resulting economic hardship.
  • Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal to address economic problems: Social Security, labor rights, and public works programs.

9. Civil Rights Movement (1950s-1960s)

  • Key Figures: Martin Luther King Jr., Rosa Parks, Malcolm X.
  • Major Events: Brown v. Board of Education (1954), Civil Rights Act (1964), and Voting Rights Act (1965).

10. Modern U.S. History (1970s-Present)

  • The Cold War: Tensions between the U.S. and the Soviet Union, the Vietnam War, and the Cuban Missile Crisis.
  • The War on Terror: The September 11, 2001 attacks and subsequent wars in Afghanistan and Iraq.
