I. Cell Structure and Function

Cells are the basic building blocks of life, and understanding their structure and function is fundamental for life science.

1.1 Cell Types

  • Prokaryotic Cells: Simple cells without a nucleus. Example: bacteria.
  • Eukaryotic Cells: More complex cells with a defined nucleus and organelles. Example: plant and animal cells.

1.2 Organelles and Their Functions

  • Nucleus: Contains the cell's genetic material (DNA) and controls cell activities.
  • Mitochondria: Produce energy through cellular respiration (the "powerhouse" of the cell).
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): Smooth ER makes lipids; rough ER has ribosomes for protein synthesis.
  • Ribosomes: Synthesize proteins.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for transport.
  • Lysosomes: Contain digestive enzymes to break down waste.
  • Chloroplasts (in plant cells): Carry out photosynthesis to produce food (glucose) from sunlight.

1.3 Cellular Processes

  • Mitosis: Cell division resulting in two genetically identical daughter cells.
  • Meiosis: Cell division that produces reproductive cells (gametes: sperm and eggs).
  • Cellular Respiration: The process by which cells generate energy (ATP) from glucose and oxygen.
  • Photosynthesis: Process by which plants convert sunlight, water, and CO2 into glucose and oxygen.

II. Genetics and Heredity

Genetics explains how traits are passed from one generation to the next.

2.1 DNA and Genes

  • DNA: The molecule that carries genetic information in all living organisms.
  • Structure: Double helix made of nucleotides (adenine, thymine, cytosine, and guanine).
  • Genes: Segments of DNA that code for proteins, which determine traits.
  • Chromosomes: DNA is organized into structures called chromosomes. Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes.

2.2 Inheritance Patterns

  • Dominant and Recessive Alleles:
    • Dominant Alleles: Only one copy is needed for the trait to be expressed (e.g., "T" for tall).
    • Recessive Alleles: Both copies must be present for the trait to be expressed (e.g., "t" for short).
  • Homozygous vs. Heterozygous:
    • Homozygous: Two identical alleles for a trait (e.g., TT or tt).
    • Heterozygous: Two different alleles for a trait (e.g., Tt).

2.3 Punnett Squares

Punnett squares help predict the probability of offspring inheriting certain traits.

  • Monohybrid Cross: A cross between two individuals examining a single trait (e.g., eye color).
  • Dihybrid Cross: A cross between two individuals examining two traits (e.g., height and color).

2.4 Genetic Disorders

  • Cystic Fibrosis: A recessive genetic disorder affecting the lungs.
  • Down Syndrome: A chromosomal disorder where there is an extra copy of chromosome 21.

III. Human Anatomy and Physiology

This section covers major body systems and their functions.

3.1 Body Systems

  • Circulatory System:
    • Composed of the heart, blood vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries), and blood.
    • Function: Transports oxygen, nutrients, and waste products throughout the body.
    • Heart: Pumps blood through the body. Divided into four chambers (left and right atria, left and right ventricles).
  • Respiratory System:
    • Composed of the lungs, trachea, bronchi, and alveoli.
    • Function: Responsible for gas exchange (oxygen in, carbon dioxide out).
  • Digestive System:
    • Composed of the mouth, esophagus, stomach, intestines, liver, and pancreas.
    • Function: Breaks down food to extract nutrients and energy.
  • Nervous System:
    • Composed of the brain, spinal cord, and nerves.
    • Function: Controls and coordinates bodily functions through electrical signals.
  • Musculoskeletal System:
    • Composed of bones, muscles, tendons, and ligaments.
    • Function: Provides structure, movement, and protection for the body.
  • Excretory System:
    • Composed of the kidneys, bladder, and urethra.
    • Function: Removes waste products and excess water from the body.

3.2 Homeostasis

Homeostasis refers to the body's ability to maintain a stable internal environment, such as temperature, pH levels, and water balance, despite external changes.

IV. Ecosystems and Environment

Ecosystems involve interactions between organisms and their environment.

4.1 Ecosystem Structure

  • Biotic Factors: Living components of an ecosystem (e.g., plants, animals, bacteria).
  • Abiotic Factors: Non-living components of an ecosystem (e.g., water, sunlight, temperature, soil).
  • Producers: Organisms (e.g., plants) that produce their own food through photosynthesis.
  • Consumers: Organisms (e.g., animals) that obtain food by eating other organisms.
    • Primary Consumers: Herbivores that eat plants.
    • Secondary Consumers: Carnivores that eat herbivores.
    • Tertiary Consumers: Top predators in the food chain.
  • Decomposers: Organisms (e.g., fungi, bacteria) that break down dead matter, returning nutrients to the soil.

4.2 Energy Flow in Ecosystems

  • Food Chains and Food Webs: Show how energy flows from producers to consumers.
  • Trophic Levels: Each level in the food chain, such as primary producers (plants), primary consumers (herbivores), and so on.
  • Energy Transfer: Only about 10% of energy is transferred to the next trophic level; the rest is lost as heat.

4.3 Biomes

  • Biomes: Large geographic areas with similar climates, plants, and animals. Examples include:
    • Tropical Rainforest: High biodiversity, warm temperatures, and lots of rainfall.
    • Desert: Dry, with extreme temperature fluctuations.
    • Temperate Forest: Moderate climate, trees shed leaves in winter.
    • Tundra: Cold, with permafrost and few plants.

V. Evolution and Natural Selection

Evolution explains how species change over time.

5.1 Natural Selection

  • Darwin’s Theory: Organisms with traits that are better suited to their environment have a higher chance of surviving and reproducing, passing those traits to offspring.
  • Adaptations: Inherited traits that improve an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its environment.
  • Survival of the Fittest: The idea that the organisms most fit for their environment will survive and reproduce more successfully than others.

5.2 Speciation

  • Allopatric Speciation: Occurs when a population is geographically separated, leading to the formation of new species.
  • Sympatric Speciation: Occurs when new species form within the same geographical area due to other factors, such as behavioral or genetic differences.

5.3 Evidence of Evolution

  • Fossil Record: Provides evidence of past life forms and their evolution over time.
  • Comparative Anatomy: The study of similarities and differences in the anatomy of different species.
    • Homologous Structures: Similar structures in different species due to common ancestry.
    • Vestigial Structures: Reduced or nonfunctional structures that were functional in ancestors (e.g., human appendix).
  • Genetic Evidence: DNA comparisons show how closely related different species are.
